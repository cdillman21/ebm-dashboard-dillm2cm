# Bayesian Analysis: Updating My Beliefs Based on Evidence

## My Initial Hypothesis
**Starting Belief (Prior Probability):** Competitive compensation + manager training will increase early-career employee retention from current 60% to 75%+ within 24 months.

**Initial Confidence:** 30%
**Why I started here:** I believed manager quality mattered for retention, but I wasn't sure if it would be enough to make a meaningful difference. I also worried that compensation alone wouldn't solve the problem. I was skeptical that combining both interventions would actually work in practice, even though it made intuitive sense.

---

## Bayesian Evidence Analysis

### Evidence Type 1: Scientific Research

**Prior Probability (before reviewing studies):** 30%

**What the Evidence Showed:**
- 5 high-quality peer-reviewed studies with sample sizes from 13,346 to 4.2M participants
- Combined compensation + manager quality approach shows 55% turnover reduction
- Pay satisfaction mediates 47% of the compensation → retention relationship (Williams et al., 2020)
- Manager quality accounts for 70% of engagement variance (Allen et al., 2010)
- Effect sizes: Cohen's d = 0.67 for compensation, d = 0.71 for manager training

**Evidence Quality:** HIGH - Multiple rigorous studies, large samples, peer-reviewed, consistent findings

**How this changed my thinking:** The scientific evidence was WAY stronger than I expected. The effect sizes are medium-to-large, the samples are massive (especially the BLS data with 4.2M workers), and multiple studies all point in the same direction. The mediator analysis showing pay SATISFACTION matters more than pay AMOUNT was a key insight I didn't have before.

**Likelihood Ratio:** 3:1 in favor (evidence strongly supports hypothesis)

**Posterior Probability (after scientific evidence):** 30% → 55%

**Confidence boost:** +25% (This evidence made me significantly more confident)

#### Strong Convergence: Manager Quality is THE Driver
**Finding:** Manager quality is the single most critical factor affecting early-career retention - far more important than compensation alone.

**Supporting Evidence Types:** ALL FOUR (Scientific, Practitioner, Organizational, Stakeholder)

- Practitioner: All 5 experts ranked manager quality as top priority

**Confidence Level:** HIGH (85%) - This is rock solid. Every single piece of evidence pointed to the same conclusion.

**Implication:** We need to invest in manager training, not just throw money at the problem. Compensation alone will fail.

#### Strong Convergence: Compensation Table Stakes
**Finding:** Competitive compensation is necessary but not sufficient - it's a "table stakes" requirement.

**Supporting Evidence Types:** Scientific, Practitioner, Organizational
- Scientific: Studies show pay SATISFACTION matters more than absolute pay level
- Practitioner: Experts say "competitive comp keeps them from leaving; good managers make them want to stay"
- Organizational: Our internal data shows employees who left had average market-rate salaries

**Confidence Level:** HIGH (80%)

**Implication:** We can't ignore compensation, but we also can't rely on it as the sole solution. It's a foundation, not a fix.

#### Moderate Convergence: Combined Approach Works Best
**Finding:** The most effective retention strategy combines competitive compensation with manager development.

**Supporting Evidence Types:** Scientific, Practitioner, Stakeholder

**Variation Notes:** Scientific evidence shows effect sizes of 25-55% improvement; practitioners report more conservative 20-30% improvement; stakeholders emphasize cost concerns.

### Divergent Findings (Where Evidence Types Disagree)

#### Major Divergence: Timeline to Results
**Issue:** How long until we see retention improvements?

**Scientific Evidence Says:** 12-18 months to see measurable impact
**Practitioner Evidence Says:** "Give it 18-24 months - culture change takes time"
**Organizational Evidence Says:** No historical data (we haven't tried this intervention before)
**Stakeholder Evidence Says:** Mixed - some companies report 6-month improvements, others say 24+ months

**Possible Explanations for Disagreement:**
- Context differences: Company size, industry, starting retention rate all affect timeline
- Timing factors: Studies measured at different intervals
- Quality differences: Scientific studies controlled for confounds; practitioner reports are anecdotal

**Resolution Approach:** Plan for 18-24 month timeline but measure quarterly. Set early indicators (engagement scores, manager feedback ratings) to track progress before retention numbers move.

#### Minor Divergence: Implementation Approach
**Issue:** Start big (organization-wide rollout) vs. start small (pilot program)?

**Scientific Evidence Says:** Doesn't address implementation strategy directly
**Practitioner Evidence Says:** "Start with a pilot, learn, then scale" (4 out of 5 experts recommended this)
**Organizational Evidence Says:** No precedent for large-scale training initiatives
**Stakeholder Evidence Says:** Pilot reduces risk and builds credibility

**Resolution Approach:** Start with pilot program (10-15 managers, 3-month duration) before full rollout. This aligns with practitioner wisdom and stakeholder preferences.

### Evidence Gaps

#### Critical Gaps
**Gap 1:** We don't have data on OUR employees' specific retention drivers
- **Impact on Decision:** Reduces confidence that our context matches the research context (generalizability concern)
- **Mitigation Strategy:** Include employee pulse survey in pilot phase - ask our employees what matters most to them

**Gap 2:** No cost-benefit analysis specific to our organization
- **Impact on Decision:** Can't confidently predict ROI or justify budget to leadership

**Gap 3:** Unknown level of manager resistance or capability

- **Mitigation Strategy:** Include manager focus group in pilot phase - get their input on training design

#### Minor Gaps
**Gap:** Limited evidence on which specific manager behaviors matter most (coaching vs. feedback vs. development opportunities)

**Mitigation Strategy:** Use scientific evidence hierarchy - focus on behaviors with strongest research support (weekly 1-on-1s, constructive feedback, career development discussions)

## Evidence-Based Problem Refinement

### Original Problem Statement
"Early-career employee retention is low at 60%, costing the organization money and talent. We need to improve retention to reduce costs and maintain institutional knowledge."

### Evidence-Refined Problem Statement  
"Early-career employees are leaving at 40% annual turnover primarily due to poor manager quality and perceived lack of development opportunities, compounded by below-market compensation. The problem is most acute in the 0-3 year tenure group. While compensation is a necessary foundation, manager quality is the primary driver of retention decisions and explains 70% of engagement variance. This is a manager capability problem disguised as a compensation problem."

**Key Refinements:**
- **Scope Changes:** Narrowed focus to 0-3 year employees (most sensitive to manager quality per scientific evidence). Broadened from "retention problem" to "manager capability and compensation problem."
- **Root Cause Updates:** Originally thought compensation was the main issue. Evidence shows manager quality is THE driver - compensation is table stakes but not sufficient.
- **Impact Reassessment:** Originally saw this as pure cost problem. Now see it as talent development and organizational capability issue. Losing early-career talent means losing future leaders.
- **Stakeholder Effects:** Originally focused on HR problem. Evidence shows this affects managers (need training), employees (need better management), and business leaders (need to invest in manager development).

## Evidence-Based Solution Development

### Original Solution Concept
"Increase compensation to market rate to improve retention."

### Evidence-Informed Solution Design
"Implement a two-part intervention: (1) Bring compensation to competitive market rates (90th percentile for key roles), and (2) Develop manager capability through structured training focused on coaching, feedback, and career development. Start with a 3-month pilot targeting 10-15 managers, measure impact on engagement and retention, then scale based on results."

**Scientific Evidence Contributions:**
- **Design Elements:** Focus training on weekly 1-on-1s, constructive feedback delivery, and career development planning (strongest research support)
- **Implementation Approaches:** Use behavioral skill-building (practice + feedback) not just classroom learning - research shows 3-5x better results
- **Success Factors:** Manager buy-in and ongoing reinforcement are critical - one-time training doesn't stick

**Practitioner Evidence Contributions:**
- **Practical Modifications:** Start with pilot program (10-15 managers) rather than organization-wide rollout - 4 out of 5 experts recommended this approach
- **Implementation Lessons:** Include managers in design process - co-creation builds ownership. Make training ongoing (monthly reinforcement) not one-and-done.
- **Avoiding Pitfalls:** Don't skip the compensation piece - experts say "you can't train your way out of a pay problem." Don't make training optional - voluntary programs have low uptake.

**Organizational Evidence Contributions:**  
- **Resource Requirements:** Based on 18% turnover cost ($50K per departure), break-even is ~2 prevented departures. Pilot should cost significantly less than $100K.
- **Timing Considerations:** Our exit interviews show most departures happen at 12-18 month mark - need to intervene earlier (6-9 months).
- **Readiness Assessment:** Limited history of manager training programs - this is new territory. Suggests need for HR partnership and executive sponsorship.

**Stakeholder Evidence Contributions:**
- **Stakeholder Support Elements:** Employees want manager quality (87% say it matters most) - this will be popular. Managers may resist if they perceive it as "you're bad at your job" training - need to frame as capability building.
- **Resistance Management:** Address cost concerns upfront with ROI analysis. Get manager input on training design to reduce resistance. Position as investment in manager career development, not remediation.
- **Communication Needs:** Be transparent about why we're doing this (retention problem) and what success looks like (25-40% improvement over 18-24 months).

## Evidence Limitations and Uncertainties

### Key Limitations Affecting Recommendations

1. **Generalizability Uncertainty:** All scientific evidence comes from other organizations - we don't know for certain our context matches theirs. Our industry, company culture, employee demographics may be different.

2. **No Internal Baseline:** We don't have data on our current manager quality or what our employees specifically value. We're assuming our situation mirrors the research contexts.

3. **Cost Estimates are External:** Industry benchmarks suggest $50K-$200K per company for manager training, but we don't know our actual costs yet.

4. **Manager Capability Unknown:** We haven't assessed our managers' current skills or willingness to participate. Some may already be excellent; others may resist training.

5. **Practitioner Evidence is Anecdotal:** While 5 experts provided consistent recommendations, this isn't peer-reviewed research. Their experiences may not generalize to our context.

### Uncertainties Requiring Monitoring

1. **Timeline Variance:** Evidence suggests 12-24 months - we need to track whether we're on the faster or slower end of that range.

2. **Manager Engagement:** If managers don't fully participate or apply learnings, intervention will fail. Monitor training attendance, skill application, employee feedback.

3. **Compensation Competitiveness:** Market rates change - need to monitor whether our "competitive" pay stays competitive over time.

4. **Employee Preferences:** What our employees value may differ from research samples - need ongoing pulse checks.

5. **Organizational Execution Capability:** We have no track record of manager development programs - need to monitor whether we can actually implement well.
